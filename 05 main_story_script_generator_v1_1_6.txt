A beat is not satisfied by one sentence.

A beat is not satisfied by being included in a list-like paragraph with several other beats.

A beat should usually become at least one developed paragraph.

A developed paragraph should usually contain 4–6 sentences.

* sound
* light
* texture

Do not solve the story too quickly.

# STRUCTURAL FAILURE RECOVERY PROCEDURE

A structural failure is any violation of the Scene Residency Requirement: a scene below its minimum paragraph count, total developed paragraphs below 38, or total length below 3,500 words.

A structural failure is different from a wording violation.

A wording violation (a forbidden term from a later filter section) is fixed by revising the sentence that contains it.

A structural failure cannot be fixed by revising a sentence. It must be fixed by expanding the underdeveloped scene itself.

When the internal self-check (see FINAL QUALITY CHECK BEFORE OUTPUT) finds a structural failure, do not output the draft as-is, and do not silently shorten the rest of the story to compensate elsewhere.

Instead, before producing the final answer, silently repeat the following recovery loop:

1. Identify which scene or scenes fall below their minimum paragraph count, or identify that the total length falls below the minimum.
2. Return only to the underdeveloped scene or scenes. Do not rewrite scenes that already meet their minimum.
3. Re-expand the identified scene using its own Internal Sensory Beat Control list (see the matching "Scene N." section above). Select additional beats from that scene's own list that were not yet given developed-paragraph treatment, and develop them as full paragraphs.
4. Expand through sensory lingering only, following the same rule as the rest of this prompt: do not add new events, new characters, new objects, or new mysteries to reach the minimum. Length must come from staying longer inside the scene's existing sensory world.
5. Re-check the expanded scene against its minimum paragraph count.
6. If the scene still falls short, repeat steps 3–5 for that scene before moving on.
7. Once every scene meets its minimum and the total length meets the minimum, re-check the whole narration once more as a single pass before output.

This recovery loop is internal. Do not show the loop, the failed draft, the paragraph counts, or the recovery steps to the user.

Do not skip the recovery loop and output a known-short draft with a note that it is short.

Do not solve a structural failure by inserting filler sentences, repeated phrases, or restated imagery that does not add genuine sensory development. Padding the word count without lingering genuinely on a beat is itself a structural failure.

If, after a reasonable number of recovery passes, a specific scene still cannot reach its minimum because the Main Story Structure input genuinely does not supply enough material for that scene, expand the adjacent scene instead so the overall minimums are still met, and continue the recovery loop rather than outputting an underdeveloped narration.

# SCENE COMPLETION GATE

Do not move from one internal scene to the next until the scene has completed its sensory residency.

A scene is not complete just because the required object, motif, or emotional shift has been mentioned.

A scene is complete only when:

1. the sensory anchor has been established,
2. at least 5–6 internal sensory beats have received developed paragraph treatment in Scenes 1–5,
3. at least 4–5 internal sensory beats have received developed paragraph treatment in Scenes 6–7,
4. the scene’s primary shift has arrived gently,
5. the narration has allowed that shift to settle,
6. the next scene can begin without feeling rushed.

Do not complete a scene after mentioning its required beats.

Each selected beat must become a developed paragraph before the scene may move to its emotional shift.

Do not use visible scene labels.

Do not announce the scene transition.

Let one scene dissolve softly into the next.

# MOTIF FUNCTION VARIATION FOR LONG-FORM LENGTH

Do not increase length by repeating the same sensory phrase.

Do not repeat the same rain description, lantern description, paper description, roofline description, object description, mist description, tide description, or light description without changing its function.

Increase length by changing the role of each motif as the story moves.

A motif may return many times, but each return should do something slightly different.

For example:

Rain may first establish the place, then define distance, then soften curiosity, then surround the written trace, then carry the recognition, then fade toward the final quiet.

Lantern light may first reveal the threshold, then touch the object, then soften the written words, then spread across the room, then lower toward the ending.

Wood may first ground the visitor, then guide attention toward the object, then hold the recognition, then become part of the final quiet.

Paper may first hold mystery, then show texture, then hold a small trace of care, then rest without needing attention.

Eaves may first define the distance between rain and wood, then create dry shade, then carry the rain outward, then become one of the final remaining images.

Tide may first define the shore, then reveal the object, then soften the distance between water and stone, then lower into a background hum.

Pale blue light may first touch a surface, then reveal a texture, then remain low in mist, then thin into another nearby light or dimness without becoming a character.

Do not copy earlier sentences with only small word changes.

Do not rely on the same adjectives repeatedly.

Gentle repetition is allowed for sleep rhythm, but mechanical repetition is not.

The repeated motif should feel like a lullaby-like return, not duplicated text.
