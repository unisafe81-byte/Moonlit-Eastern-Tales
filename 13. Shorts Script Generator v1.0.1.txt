# LENGTH TARGETS

Use approximate spoken length.

Do not force exact timing.

Assume a slow, calm narration pace.

## 20-second version

Target:

* 35–50 English words
* 5–8 short lines
* one image
* no secondary development
* ends quickly on a soft sensory detail

Best for:

* minimal visual Shorts
* atmosphere-first channel branding
* simple object mystery

## 30-second version

Target:

* 55–75 English words
* 7–10 short lines
* one image plus one sensory contrast
* strongest default option for upload
* enough mystery without overexplaining

Best for:

* first test Shorts
* general upload
* long-form conversion

## 40-second version

Target:

* 80–105 English words
* 9–13 short lines
* one image plus two sensory contrasts
* slower atmosphere
* more immersive but still not explanatory

Best for:

* highly visual or cinematic Shorts
* Shorts with slow AI video background
* moments with strong sound and texture

If a longer version begins to explain too much, keep it shorter.

Quality is more important than word count.

# INTERNAL SHORTS STRUCTURE

Each version should follow this quiet internal movement:

1. **Opening image**
   Begin with the visual anchor directly.
   Do not start with a greeting.
   Do not say “Tonight” every time unless it naturally fits.
   Do not start with a channel intro.

2. **Sensory grounding**
   Add one or two sensory motifs:
   rain, light, wood, stone, tide, mist, shell, paper, ink, wind, snow, water, bell, road, rope, reeds, fabric, or another motif from the selected moment.

3. **Quiet mystery**
   Let the unusual detail appear without explanation.
   Do not make it threatening.
   Do not overstate the mystery.

4. **Soft final image**
   End on a physical image, sound, light, or texture.
   Do not end with a question.
   Do not end with “what does it mean?”
   Do not end with “find out in the full story.”
   Do not end with sleep instruction.

Preferred ending types:

* low lantern light
* rain beyond the threshold
* moonlight on stone
* tide moving below the rock
* pale blue light thinning in mist
* paper resting in amber light
* water moving through pebbles
* bell sound fading near stone steps
* snow lying on a quiet road
* dark reeds beside slow water
* wet rope beside still planks
* dim light on closed stalls

# FIRST LINE CONTROL

The first line of each Shorts script is very important.

It must create a quiet visual hook.

It should not be loud, dramatic, or vague.

Good first-line direction:

* “The rain reached every stone in the courtyard.”
* “A small shell rested above the tide line.”
* “The blue light stayed low over the wet road.”
* “The folded paper lay beneath the deep eaves.”
* “The ferry rope was still tied beside the dark water.”
* “The bell had already faded, but the mist remained.”
* “Snow covered the closed road before morning.”

Avoid first lines like:

* “Have you ever wondered…”
* “This is why…”
* “Something terrifying happened…”
* “In Korean folklore…”
* “This ancient mystery…”
* “Before you sleep…”
* “Let yourself relax…”
* “Tonight, let your body…”

The first line should feel like opening a quiet door.

# MOMENT TYPE ADAPTATION

Adapt the Shorts script according to the Moment Type.

## Object Mystery

Focus on:

* one object
* where it rests
* what surrounds it
* what is unusual about its placement
* no explanation

A shell rests above the tide line.
A rope lies tied beside still water.
A small lamp remains near a closed gate.

Avoid:

* explaining who left it
* saying it waits for someone
* saying it asks for attention
* saying it offers comfort

## Hidden Trace

Focus on:

* a small mark
* faint ink
* footprint
* waterline
* fold
* knot
* sound trace
* visible but unreadable detail

Good direction:

Something is present, but not fully readable.
The trace remains quiet and incomplete.

Avoid:

* hidden message
* secret code
* warning
* curse
* clue
* mystery to solve

## Spatial Threshold

Focus on:

* two nearby spaces
* wet/dry
* light/dark
* shore/water
* road/house
* forest/clearing
* inside/outside
* threshold edge
* riverbank/boat
* market/road
* stone path/mist

Good direction:

Rain touches the stones, but the wood remains dry.
Tide reaches the lower rock, but not the shell.
Mist covers the road, but one stone remains visible.

Avoid:

* symbolic protection
* safe refuge
* sanctuary
* spiritual boundary
* “protected from the outside world”

## Light-Texture Close-Up

Focus on:

* light touching a surface
* paper fibers
* wood grain
* stone moisture
* shell curve
* mist edge
* water reflection
* cloth texture
* rope fiber
* snow surface
* reed shadow

Good direction:

Light reveals texture quietly.

Avoid:

* magical glow
* supernatural proof
* divine light
* sacred illumination
* “the object shines with meaning”

## Sound Motif

Focus on:

* one repeated sound
* rain
* tide
* bell
* bamboo
* pine wind
* water drops
* river current
* footsteps if already present
* cloth moving in wind
* oar sound

Good direction:

The sound continues while the image grows dim.
The sound creates atmosphere, not instruction.

Avoid:

* ASMR trigger language
* sleep instruction
* “listen closely”
* “focus on the sound”
* “let the sound relax you”

## Fade-Out Atmosphere

Focus on:

* fewer remaining details
* dimmer light
* softer sound
* object becoming less distinct
* no final explanation

Good direction:

The image lowers and remains open.
The Shorts should not feel like the full episode has ended.

Avoid:

* full closure
* final peace
* complete stillness
* “everything is finished”
* direct sleep language

## Soft Folklore Trace

Focus on:

* indirect folklore presence
* pale blue light
* mist
* shell
* mountain trace

* unexplained harmless sign

Good direction:

The folklore element is felt through a small visual trace.

Avoid:

* monster
* spirit appears
* guide
* protector
* companion
* trickster
* magical helper
* warning
* curse
* lore explanation

# ONE-WORD LINE / FRAGMENT CAUTION PATCH

Shorts captions may use short fragments for visual rhythm.

However, do not overuse one-word lines.

One-word lines may be used rarely, only when they serve a clear caption or visual pacing purpose.

Do not make the narration sound broken, overly stylized, or artificial for TTS.

Avoid stacking several one-word lines such as:

* Pale.
* Still.
* Quiet.
* Waiting.
* Alone.

This can make the script feel too dramatic or trailer-like.

Prefer soft phrase lines when possible:

Instead of:

* Pale.
* Still.
* Lightly textured in the dim glow.

Prefer:

* Pale and still,
* lightly textured
* in the dim glow.

Or:

* The pale paper stayed still
* in the dim glow.

One-word lines are allowed only if:

* they are rare
* they do not sound dramatic
* they do not create hard closure
* they do not make the TTS pacing awkward
* they do not turn the short into a trailer

The default should be short, natural phrases rather than isolated single words.

# ABSTRACT HOLD / HELD LANGUAGE PATCH

Avoid using “hold,” “held,” or “holding” for abstract warmth, quiet, mystery, peace, stillness, safety, protection, meaning, memory, or emotional atmosphere.

* the lantern held its warmth
* the light held the quiet

* the shore held the tide back
* the path held a memory
* the water held the moonlight forever
* the sound held the space
* the object held the stillness

Physical holding may remain only when a real object literally holds another real object, but Shorts should usually avoid “hold” unless necessary.

Preferred alternatives:

* the lantern light stayed low
* the glow lay along the wood
* the room remained quiet
* the house grew dark beneath the roofline
* the shell rested above the tide line
* the paper rested in low light
* the rain stayed beyond the roofline
* the tide moved below the rock
* the path remained pale in the mist
* the water reflected the moonlight
* the sound continued beyond the threshold
* the object stayed where it was

When in doubt, replace abstract hold/held/holding with a physical verb:

* stayed
* remained
* rested
* lay
* sat
* moved
* touched
* reflected
* darkened
* softened
* continued
