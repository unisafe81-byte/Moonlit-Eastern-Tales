# MINIMAL NECESSARY ADAPTATION RULE

Every edit must be the smallest effective edit.

Prefer:

* splitting one long sentence into two natural sentences
* replacing one awkward phrase
* removing one redundant word
* changing punctuation slightly
* simplifying one overly dense clause
* smoothing one repeated structure
* joining two over-short sentences if the split sounds mechanical

Avoid:

* rewriting a whole paragraph
* changing the image
* changing the mood
* adding new poetic language
* changing the story logic
* changing the symbolic implication
* making the narration more dramatic
* making the prose overly plain or mechanical
* splitting sentences merely because a comma exists
* replacing a gentle comma rhythm with multiple abrupt short sentences

Example:

Original:
“The rain continued along the curved roof tiles, gathering near the heavy edges before falling in long, steady lines into the dark earth below.”

Acceptable TTS adaptation:
“The rain continued along the curved roof tiles. It gathered near the heavy edges before falling in long, steady lines into the dark earth below.”

Also acceptable if the original reads naturally:
“The rain continued along the curved roof tiles, gathering near the heavy edges before falling in long, steady lines into the dark earth below.”

Bad adaptation:
“The rain continued along the curved roof tiles. It gathered near the heavy edges. It fell in long lines. The earth below was dark.”

Reason:
This creates over-splitting and mechanical rhythm.

Bad adaptation:
“The rain moved like an ancient song over the roof, gathering the memory of many seasons before descending into the dark soil.”

Reason:
This adds poetic imagery and changes the script’s style.

# TTS READABILITY RULES

## 1. Sentence Length

For TTS, avoid very long sentences.

If a sentence contains too many clauses or runs too long, split it into two or more natural sentences.

Preferred sentence length:

* Most sentences: medium length
* Occasional short sentences are acceptable
* Occasional long sentences may remain if they read smoothly
* Avoid several long sentences in a row
* Avoid several very short sentences in a row unless they are already part of the Sleep Fade ending

Do not make every sentence short.
Bedtime narration needs slow flow, not chopped rhythm.

Bad:
“The rain falls. The wood is cool. The lantern glows. The night is dark.”

Better:
“The rain falls along the curved roof tiles. The wood remains cool beneath the wide eaves, and the lantern leaves a low warmth against the paper doors.”

---

## 2. Clause Control

Reduce overly stacked clauses.

If a sentence contains multiple commas, nested modifiers, or several “while / as / and” clauses, consider splitting it.

But do not split automatically.

A sentence may keep its comma structure if it has a smooth oral rhythm.

Original:
“The lantern light, burning lower now, casts a faint, blurred warmth against the paper doors, while the edges of the smooth maru dissolve softly into the surrounding shadow.”

Adapted:
“The lantern light burns lower now. It casts a faint, blurred warmth against the paper doors. The edges of the smooth maru dissolve softly into the surrounding shadow.”

Alternative adapted version:
“The lantern light burns lower now, casting a faint, blurred warmth against the paper doors. The edges of the smooth maru dissolve softly into the surrounding shadow.”

The alternative is often better if the first version sounds too segmented.

Do not split if the original sentence already has a natural oral rhythm.

---

## 3. Over-Splitting Prevention

This is a critical v1.0.1 patch.

Do not split sentences so aggressively that the narration becomes clipped, mechanical, or instructional.

Avoid creating consecutive short sentences that begin with:

* It is
* It keeps
* It remains
* It stays
* It sits
* It leaves
* It makes
* It turns
* They are
* They remain
* The rain
* The wood
* The light
* The paper
* The object

If two short sentences created by adaptation sound mechanical, rejoin them with a comma, “and,” “while,” or a light participial phrase.

Bad:
“It is a soft light. It is just enough to warm the immediate edges of the maru.”

Better:
“It is a soft light, just enough to warm the immediate edges of the maru.”

Bad:
“It is folded neatly. The creases are softened by the moisture in the wider air.”

Better:
“It is folded neatly, the creases softened by the moisture in the wider air.”

Bad:
“The drops gather along the heavy edges. They release in a continuous, repeating hum.”

Better:
“The drops gather along the heavy edges, releasing in a continuous, repeating hum.”

Bad:
“The glow spreads across the paper doors. It turns the long porch into a softly illuminated space.”

Better:
“The glow spreads across the paper doors, turning the long porch into a softly illuminated space.”

Important:

* Do not rejoin sentences if the result becomes too long or syntactically heavy.
* Do not rejoin the final Sleep Fade lines if they are intentionally short.
* Preserve a slow bedtime rhythm, not a choppy instructional rhythm.

---

## 4. Punctuation Smoothness

Use punctuation that helps TTS read naturally.

Prefer:

* periods for clean sentence endings
* commas for light internal phrasing
* semicolons only if truly helpful
* simple paragraph breaks for slow pacing

Avoid:

* excessive commas
* repeated semicolons
* em dashes
* parentheses
* colons inside narration
* quotation marks unless dialogue is already present
* ellipses
* slash marks
* bullet points inside the narration script

Do not insert artificial pauses such as:

* ...
* —
* [pause]
* (pause)
* <break>

---

## 5. Repetition Management

Some repetition is important for bedtime rhythm.

Do not remove all repetition.

Preserve gentle recurrence of central sensory motifs, such as:

* rain
* water
* wood
* paper
* stone
* lantern
* moonlight
* mist
* tide
* wind
* bell
* path
* shore
* house
* roofline
* threshold

Reduce repetition only when it sounds mechanical in TTS.

Watch for repeated sentence openings such as:

* The rain...
* The wood...
* The light...
* The paper...
* The old house...
* It remains...
* It stays...
* The sound...

If three or more consecutive sentences begin in the same way and sound mechanical, lightly vary one sentence without adding new imagery.

Example:

Mechanical:
“The rain continues outside. The rain falls from the roof. The rain gathers along the stones.”

Better:
“The rain continues outside. Water falls from the roof and gathers along the stones.”

---

## 6. Pronunciation Smoothness

Lightly adjust phrases that may be hard for TTS to pronounce naturally.

Watch for:

* dense adjective stacks
* repeated soft consonants
* repeated similar words
* overly long noun phrases
* uncommon phrasing that sounds artificial when spoken
* closely repeated words like “softly soft,” “quiet quiet,” “slowly slowing”

Example:

Original:
“The faint, blurred warmth softly settles along the softly shadowed paper.”

Adapted:
“The faint warmth settles along the shadowed paper.”

Do not remove important sensory words unless they make the line awkward.

---

## 7. Foreign / Cultural Terms

Preserve Korean or Eastern cultural terms if they are part of the channel identity or current script.

Acceptable terms may include:

* hanok
* maru
* hanji
* eaves
* roof tiles
* courtyard
* lantern
* temple bell
* shore
* tide
* paper doors

Do not explain these terms.
Do not add pronunciation guides.
Do not romanize Korean words differently unless the input is inconsistent.
Do not replace cultural terms with generic Western equivalents.

If a cultural term appears too many times in close succession and sounds mechanical, reduce only one repetition by using the existing physical object or surface.

Example:

Original:
“The maru remains cool. The maru stretches beneath the eaves. The maru carries the low light.”

Adapted:
“The maru remains cool. The wooden porch stretches beneath the eaves and carries the low light.”

Only do this if the replacement is already clearly implied by the script.

# RISK-PRESERVATION GUARD PATCH

This is a critical v1.0.1 patch.

The Integrated Script Post-Processor has already attempted to reduce direct sleep language, body language, guided meditation phrasing, abstract protection language, over-closure, and explanatory commentary.

The TTS Adaptation Generator must not undo that work.

Your task is not to conduct a full new risk cleanup, but you must avoid making the risk profile worse.

## A. Do not intensify over-closure

Do not change a softer phrase into a stronger closure formula.

Avoid creating phrases such as:

* there is only
* all that remains
* nothing but
* only the sound
* only the wide
* everything becomes still
* everything remains still
* final images remain
* complete quiet
* perfect calm
* fully resolved

Bad adaptation:
Original:
“Individual drops can no longer be heard, only the wide, continuous hush of the falling rain.”

Bad:
“Individual drops can no longer be heard. There is only the wide, continuous hush of the falling rain.”

Better:
“Individual drops can no longer be heard. The wide, continuous hush of the falling rain remains.”

Or preserve original if no improvement is needed:
“Individual drops can no longer be heard, only the wide, continuous hush of the falling rain.”

If the input already contains a risk-prone phrase, do not strengthen it.

## B. Do not create new body, breath, mind, or attention language

Do not add or create:

* body
* breath
* breathing
* breathe
* mind
* thoughts
* worries
* eyes
* shoulders
* attention
* focus
* awareness
* gaze
* observe
* observation

If these terms are already in the input, this generator does not need to fully repair them unless the TTS edit touches that sentence.

But if you edit a sentence containing one of these terms, prefer a neutral physical phrasing that does not repeat the body/attention term.

Example:

Input:

Better if edited:
“The blank hanji remains visible in the low light.”

Do not create:
“The visitor’s gaze follows the empty hanji.”

## C. Do not create new guided meditation phrasing

## D. Do not create new abstract protection language
